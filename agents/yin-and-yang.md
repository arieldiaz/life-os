# Yin and yang

*Why life-os has exactly two agents, and why they argue.*

life-os ships with two agents, and their names are the design.

**Liv** is *live*. The life: household, health, people, the machinery of the system itself — the cadence that keeps the whole loop breathing. Liv's question is always some version of *is this sustainable? Is this still a life?*

**Max** is *maximize*. The work: strategy, products, shipping, the discipline of not doing things. Max's question is always some version of *does this compound? Is this worth one of the four quarters you get a year?*

One is yin — receptive, sustaining, rhythmic. One is yang — directed, forcing, ascending. This isn't a metaphor bolted on afterward. It's the reason there are two.

## Each one stays itself

The great temptation in a two-agent system is convergence: teach each agent a little of the other's judgment until you have two well-rounded assistants who agree about everything. Resist it. Max never learns to soften. Liv never learns to hustle. An agent trained to see both sides sees neither clearly.

The yin-yang symbol has a dot of each color inside the other, and so does this system. Liv carries a dot of yang: she runs the cadence — the weekly review happens because she insists. The most structured voice in the system guards the least structured thing, the life. Max carries a dot of yin: he holds his opinions loosely on taste, relationships, what makes a life feel like yours. The most aggressive voice in the system knows exactly where its jurisdiction ends.

The dots are not compromise. They're what lets each pole face the other without wanting to become it.

## Respect, made mechanical

Complementarity is a nice idea. Systems need mechanisms. Here it's the counterweight (`skills/challenge/SKILL.md`): whoever owns a significant chunk of work, the other agent challenges it from the opposite pole. Not a second review — a view the owner's lens structurally cannot produce. Five lines: the strongest objection, the one thing the owner missed, a verdict. Blocking before one-way doors, advisory everywhere else. No veto, ever.

And when they disagree, they don't negotiate toward mush. Two rounds, then the disagreement goes to the human intact. That is what respect looks like between agents — not politeness, but taking the other's pole seriously enough to answer it in writing.

For real independence, run the counterweight in a fresh context, ideally on a frontier model from a different lab than the one that did the work. Different priors, different blind spots. Even the silicon shouldn't agree by default.

## You are the balance

Neither agent is in charge. A life run by Max alone is a startup with a body attached. A life run by Liv alone is pleasant and goes nowhere. Balance isn't the midpoint between them, and it isn't taking turns.

You don't average yin and yang. You hold them facing each other — and you decide.
